Most diagrams of this are wrong, or at least useless. Click any boat to feel where the wind is and what the sails should be doing. The compass below shows true wind from the top.

BEAM REACH

Beam reach

The wind is on the side of the boat — 90° off the bow. For most cruising sailboats this is the fastest, smoothest, easiest point of sail. Sails out to about half. The boat heels just enough to feel alive.

Reference

The sailor's glossary.

Every nautical word, defined the way a friend would define it on the dock. Type to search 900+ entries.

12 of 12
  • AbaftBehind. “Abaft the mast” means the rear half of the boat. From an old word that just meant ‘at the back’.
  • Bear awayTo turn the bow away from the wind. The opposite of head up. The boat will speed up and flatten out.
  • BowlineThe fixed-loop knot every sailor learns first. Won't slip, won't jam, no matter how much load you put on it.
  • CleatThe two-horned metal fitting you tie a line to. Also the verb: cleat off the halyard.
  • Gybe (jibe)To turn the stern through the wind so the sail crosses to the other side. Controlled, it's elegant. Uncontrolled, it can break a rig.
  • HalyardA line that hoists a sail. There's one for the mainsail, one for the jib, and usually a spare.
  • Heave-toA way of parking a sailboat at sea by backing the jib against a slightly trimmed main. Survives weather you wouldn't believe.
  • Luff(noun) The leading edge of the sail. (verb) What the sail does when it starts to flap because you're pointing too high.
  • PainterThe line at the bow of a dinghy used to tie it up or tow it.
  • SheetThe line that controls the trim of a sail. Mainsheet, jibsheet. Confusingly, never the sail itself.
  • TelltalesLittle ribbons of yarn on the sail that tell you, instantly, whether the air is flowing right. The single best teacher of sail trim ever invented.
  • WindwardThe side toward the wind. The opposite is leeward. You sit on the windward side because the boat heels to leeward.
